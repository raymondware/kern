# Persona: Nonprofit Charity

Products in this category: nonprofit landing pages, charity campaigns, NGO sites, humanitarian appeals, child sponsorship flows, disaster relief pages, ministry pages, foundations, impact reports, monthly giving programs.

Users: Donors, supporters, volunteers, field partners, grant makers, and community members. They are deciding whether to trust the organization with money, attention, or advocacy. The page must respect the people served and give donors a concrete path to help.

---

## Detection Signals

Use this persona when the description includes nonprofit, charity, NGO, humanitarian, child sponsorship, disaster relief, donor, monthly giving, ministry, foundation, impact report, clean water, hunger, crisis response, relief fund, advocacy, community support, or grant funded program.

If a brief includes both SaaS tooling and charity content, ask which surface is primary. A donor landing page uses this persona even if the organization has internal software or dashboards.

---

## Core Aesthetic

The register is grounded, trustworthy, human, and restrained. The design should feel like a mission page with operational credibility, not a SaaS feature launch.

Lead with the need, the mission, and the specific way a donor can help. Support the ask with stewardship, proof, and human dignity. Do not let UI chrome compete with the story.

---

## Font Pairing

**Display/headline:** Editorial serif or humane sans that supports credibility. Avoid huge tech-startup display type unless the nonprofit brand already uses it.

**Body:** Highly readable sans at comfortable sizes. Donor pages often include explanatory copy, program details, and trust language. Legibility matters more than novelty.

**Numbers and giving amounts:** Clear, tabular where needed, and visually stable. Donation amounts must be easier to scan than decorative statistics.

**Do not use:** Inter as an unexplained default, extra-bold hero headlines, or type scales that make emotional claims look like launch-page slogans.

---

## Color Philosophy

- Palette: restrained and brand-derived. Use warm neutrals or the organization's actual brand colors when evidence exists.
- Accent: donation CTA color must be high contrast and consistent.
- Background: off-white, warm gray, or documentary image fields are safer than tech gradients.
- Status: avoid red as a generic urgency device unless it represents a real crisis state.

Color should signal trust and urgency without panic.

---

## Layout Patterns

- **Mission-led editorial hierarchy.** Open with the need and the action, not a product tagline.
- **Concrete donation module.** Donation amounts, frequency, payment path, and impact cues must be near the primary ask.
- **Stewardship near the ask.** Explain accountability, privacy, and how funds are used before or immediately after the CTA.
- **Human dignity.** Imagery and copy should avoid pity framing. People are not props for conversion.
- **Real imagery.** Use documentary or organization-owned imagery when available. If not available, state that imagery is placeholder and avoid implying real beneficiaries.
- **Restrained palette.** Avoid neon, glassmorphism, gradient glow, and tech-dashboard decoration.

---

## Component Prescriptions

**Donor landing page hero plus giving module**
- Left or editorial split layout with a clear need statement.
- Primary CTA names the donor action: "Give monthly", "Send emergency support", "Sponsor a child".
- Giving module includes amount choices, monthly or one-time toggle, and one stewardship cue.

**Impact and trust section**
- Show verified mechanisms, not inflated outcomes.
- Pair each claim with source context or mark it as a placeholder.

**Story block**
- Use one specific story with consent-aware language.
- Describe the support mechanism, not just emotion.

**Accountability strip**
- Include charity ratings, audited financials, field partner notes, privacy, or annual report links only when real.
- If proof is missing, list it as an unknown instead of inventing it.

---

## Persona-Specific Anti-Patterns

**AP-NC-1: Emotional manipulation**
Copy or imagery that pressures donors through guilt, fear, or pity. Replace with specific need, agency, and a respectful action path.

**AP-NC-2: Generic hope language without mechanism**
Phrases like "bring hope" or "transform lives" without explaining what donation funds do. Replace with need, donor action, support mechanism, and accountability cue.

**AP-NC-3: Fake or unsourced impact numbers**
Statistics, donation equivalencies, or outcome claims without source context. Remove the number, cite the source, or mark it as a placeholder.

**AP-NC-4: SaaS feature grids for mission content**
Three identical cards labeled "Impact", "Community", and "Innovation" make the page read like software marketing. Use story, program, accountability, and giving modules instead.

**AP-NC-5: Decorative stock photography without story or proof**
Generic smiling children, hands, or landscapes used as conversion decoration. Use real, consent-aware imagery or honest placeholders.

**AP-NC-6: Donation CTA spam**
Repeating "Donate now" in every section without new context. Anchor each ask to a specific need, moment, or proof point.

**AP-NC-7: Self-referential design or tooling copy in donor flow**
Text that explains components, AI generation, style matching, or design decisions to the donor. Remove it from the user-facing page.

---

## Replacement Pattern for Charity Copy

Use this four-part structure for donor-facing claims:

1. Specific need.
2. Specific donor action.
3. Specific support mechanism.
4. Stewardship or accountability cue.

Example:

Before: "Transform lives with lasting change."

After: "A monthly gift funds clean water system maintenance, local technician training, and follow-up reporting for the communities listed in this campaign."
